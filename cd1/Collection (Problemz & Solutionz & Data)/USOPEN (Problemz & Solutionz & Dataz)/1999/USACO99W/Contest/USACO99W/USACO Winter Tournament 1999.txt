General comments:

The score results are shown below.  I used a program to determine how
much each problem was worth and how much each test case was worth.
Basically, it counts how many people got test cases and problems wrong
and uses that to estimate a difficulty and choose a weight.

Consider this (partial) result line for the Silver Division
Problem 1:

               x--45 6 30 

Given these symbols:

pts     sum   meaning              pts sym meaning
100% 1..9a..f correct               0   x  too slow
80%     -    correct but slow      0   .  incorrect
60%     *    half correct          0   n  no run, no answer, no compile

That means he was too slow on test case one, correct but slow
on test cases 2 and 3, and was correct for cases 4, 5, and 6.
Here's how the 30 points out of 39 was determined:

First the problem weights were consulted:

Problem 1:  1: 6 2: 5 3: 6 4: 5 5: 6 6:11   Total: 39

Then the percents were tabulated along with the weights for his score:

x  0.6 x  6 ->  0.0
-  0.8 x  5 ->  4.0
-  0.8 x  6 ->  4.8
4  1.0 x  5 ->  5.0
5  1.0 x  6 ->  6.0
6  1.0 x 11 -> 11.0

which sums to 30.8, which was displayed as 30.  Subsequently,
that 30.8 problem points out of 39 possible points was multiplied
by the problem's value (396.6) to calculate his total points
for problem 1:

30.8/39  * 396.6 -> about 313.2 points towards the 1,000 point total.
